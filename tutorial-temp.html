Gene Testing Guide

Overview

Every cat has a hidden gene code.
This gene code holds information on a cat, between how it looks, what growth pattern it has, and what genes it carries but does not show, and can pass onto offspring.
A cat's gene code can be revealed by using a Family Tree specialty item on the cat. Using a Family Tree on every cat in a village quickly grows expensive, though, which is where gene testing comes into play.
Using the Beans Sandbox, you can input two cats' IDs and preview what their potential offspring would look like.
With the right combination of cats with known gene strings, we can deduce most of a cat's gene string without spending a single Family Tree.

The Gene String Explained

An example of a cat's gene string looks like:

[C] [NN] [SS] [BBFF4] [YYTS] [YY3C] [AA] [RR]

Each bracket [] is one category of genes. The pattern always has the following categories, with different contents depending on the cat. For the previous example, the categories are:
  • [C] - Species
  • [NN] - Wind
  • [SS] - Fur Length
  • [BBFF4] - Colors
  • [YYTS] - Pattern
  • [YY3C] - White Markings
  • [AA] - Growth
  • [RR] - Accent Color
For each category, there are individual alleles, represented by a single letter. Some alleles stand on their own and whatever gene is there is the one that the cat displays, like the species allele [C].
Others have chromosome subcategories, where dominant and recessive genes pair together and display depending on the combination, like the wind alleles [NN].
In some of the more complicated subcategories, there are multiple chromosomal subcategories, like in the colors category [BBFF4], where you could separate it further into [(BB)(FF)(4)]
*All genes listed are dominant unless they specify that they are recessive

[Species]

  • C - Not-Cat
  • M - Mercat

[Wind]

  • N - North
  • S - South
  • O - Null (recessive)
*Trade wind cats happen when a cat inherits both a North and a South gene [NS]

[Fur Length]

  • S - Shorthair
  • L - Longhair (recessive)

[Colors]

(Color Group)

  • O - Orange Group
  • B - Black Group

(Color Diluteness)

  • F - Full
  • D - Dilute (recessive)

(Color Density)

  • 1-4 Color Density
*For North wind cats, only the first color gene is displayed on the cat
*For South wind cats, only the second color gene is displayed on the cat
*For Trade wind cats, if the two color genes are the same ([OO] or [BB]), the cat is a Watercolor coat. If the color genes are different ([OB] or [BO]), the cat is a Tortoiseshell coat
*For Null wind cats, all color information is disregarded, and the coat color is replaced with the Snow color.
For the sake of simplicity, all cats here are assumed to be north wind
The ? allele is a placeholder to indicate that we don't know or need to know the allele- any valid allele can apply there

[Growth Pattern]

  • A - Fastest
  • B - Moderate
  • C - Slowest
*With Growth genes, the order of the alleles does matter for the output
*Growth genes cannot be gene tested through the Beans Sandbox. There are ways to test cats for their Growth gene, but sometimes you can't know without actually breeding the cat or revealing its genes.
  • [AA] - Very Early
  • [AB] - Early
  • [AC] - Decreasing
  • [BA] - Arch
  • [BB] - Steady
  • [BC] - Dip
  • [CA] - Very Late
  • [CB] - Late
  • [CC] - Increasing

[Accent Color]

  • R - Red
  • B - Blue
  • Y - Yellow
  • L - Black
  • [RR] Red + Red = Ruby
  • [RB] or [BR] Red + Blue = Violet
  • [RY] or [YR] Red + Yellow = Amber
  • [RL] or [LR] Red + Black = Pink
  • [BB] Blue + Blue = Blue
  • [BY] or [YB] Blue + Yellow = Green
  • [BL] or [LB] Blue + Black = Indigo
  • [YY] Yellow + Yellow = Gold
  • [YL] or [LY] Yellow + Black = Teal
  • [LL] Black + Black = Black

When dealing with dominant and recessive genes, dominant genes will always dominate the result. For example, if a cat had [SO] for its wind genes, the cat would be a South cat, as the dominant S (South) takes priority over the recessive O (Null).
In order for a recessive gene to display on a cat, the cat needs to only have copies of the recessive gene. For wind genes, for example, the cat needs to have [OO] for its wind to be Null. Any N (North) or S (South) would take over if it was present.
In most cases, the order of the genes does not matter. Main exceptions of this rule are Color Group allele order in north and south cats, and Growth allele order.

What the Not-Cataloguer does for you

When you paste a cat's page into the Not-Cataloguer, it catalogues all publicly available data on your cat, including its appearance information.
The Not-Cataloguer then will then give you an incomplete gene code based on what information was publicly available.
How does it do that? You could do it too! Let's explain how to do it manually so every step of geneing cats is transparent

  1. Starting off with the species - under Appearance, we see that her species is listed as Not-cat. For our Species section, we mark sunny as [C]
  2. Then we move onto wind. Sunny's wind is listed under Basic Data, and she is a trade wind. Since we know trade winds always have both North and South wind genes, we know her wind gene is [NS] (or [SN], it doesn't matter what order it's in, but for convenience I usually list them in order of dominance where possible. In this case both genes are dominant, so I'll just say arbitrarily her wind gene is [NS])
  3. For fur length, Sunny's fur is listed as Longhair. Since we know Longhair is recessive, Sunny has to have 2 longhair genes for it to show. Her Fur Length gene is [LL]
  4. Onto colors- probably the most tricky category. We note that Sunny is a Black-Chocolate Watercolor. In watercolors and tortoiseshells, the first color listed before the dash (-) is their first fur color, so Sunny has Black fur. Black fur is caused by a black gene, not orange, and we know watercolors have the same color gene twice, so the beginning of the color gene is [BB]. Since black is a full color, we know that Sunny has at least 1 copy of the dominant Full gene. She could have a recessive Dilute gene that isn't showing. We can't find that out without revealing her genes or gene testing her, so for now, we mark her color genes down as [BBF?]. Black is color density 4, so the final color gene code we get is [BBF?4]
  5. For pattern, we note that Sunny's pattern is Cloudpoint. Since she's not solid, we know that she has at least 1 dominant "yes" display gene, so we start the gene code off with [Y?]. She could carry a no, we don't know, hence the ?. If we go and reference what genes Cloudpoint needs to display, we find it needs Marble and Point genes, so her pattern genes come out to [Y?MP]
  6. Sunny's white markings are listed as "Nose & Toes / P2". First off, we know that she is showing white marks at all, which means she has at least 1 "yes" gene to show white marks, so we start off with [Y?] for her white marks gene. Then, it's easy to determine the rest, as it lists it right there in the "P2". She has level 2 white, and her white is piebald type, so her full white markings gene is [Y?2P]
  7. We don't know Sunny's Growth genes since that isn't publicly available, so we write that her growth genes are [??]
  8. We don't know Sunny's accent genes either, since she isn't a Mercat, so her accent genes are [??] as well
  9. Finally, we put all the information we got together. Sunny's incomplete gene code, exactly as the website would have found it on the Add Cat page, is [C] [NS] [LL] [BBF?4] [Y?MP] [Y?2P] [??] [??]

Using the Beans Sandbox to Gene Test Cats

Once we have an incomplete gene code, like Sunny's from the example ([C] [NS] [LL] [BBF?4] [Y?MP] [Y?2P] [??] [??]), we want to fill in as many of those question marks (?) as possible
The Not-Cataloguer makes this process fairly simple. It gives you a list of tests to take, you select one, and it tells you the ID of a cat that will help you gene test your cat, and tells you what you're supposed to be looking for in the bean results.
In Sunny's gene code, the first missing gene we have is a Dilute gene. To test if Sunny carries Dilute, we want to put her in the Beans Sandbox with a cat that is Dilute, and if any of her beans are dilute, she must be carrying dilute.
In the Not-Cataloguer, you would just press a button here to say if there are dilutes. In a manual geneing example though, we would manually adjust her Color gene section to [BBFD4] if she carries dilute, and [BBFF4] if she doesn't.
When geneing manually, it's important to understand the logic behind what you're checking for. With that in mind, we'll explain how exactly beans inherit genes from their parents.

Punnet Squares and What That Means For Your Cats

You might remember Punnet Squares from your biology class if you had one. Basically, when genes are being passed down, each parent will pass down one copy of an allele they have.
Which allele the parent passes down is randomly selected from their available alleles. For example, a cat with [SL] alleles in their Fur Length category would have a 50% chance of passing down S, and 50% chance of passing down L.
To illustrate all possible combinations that can be passed down, we draw a Punnet Square. You can draw one pretty easily with the shape of a plus, a window, or a cross.
